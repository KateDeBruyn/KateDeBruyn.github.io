Reflection: Website Iteration One

The webiste thus far is made up of very simple HTML and CSS but is backed by the content we've enaged with this year. A reading that struck out the most to me was McLuhan's "The Medium Is The Message". It made me think about how my webiste, regardless of its text and imagery, represents me. I feel that the colour scheme I've gone with sort of speaks to a computer-esque aesthetic which is important for me to portray because of the field of study and industry I'm in. That being "digital". I thought about the anatomy of the website and what I want it to represent. After coming across CSS grid tutorial I realised that this would be a great way to maintain consistency throughout my website. Consistency in design has the power to familiarise the viewer with a certain brand or aesthetic and to link that to their idea of the developer... me. That's important when building a portfolio. You want your audience to be able to recognise that even the website itself is the portfolio. The medium is the message. Does my website and its content scream keightyrose? Is the message "third year student"? But the choice to use a somewhat consistent design scheme is honestly more selfish. Design often reflects the designers personal ongoings; what is inspiring them, influencing them, holding them back, spiralling them into despair, showing that despair… And that's maybe why I choice consistency because it is one of my biggest struggles. It's hard to know if it's mental health related or just straight up laziness, and it's even harder to admit and overcome both. So at first glance it may have this minimalistic appeal (something I'm not against but if you had to see my house…), some may even think it's boring, but it's what represents me right now. I wanted neatly contained bite-sized pieces of information that are easy to take in.I also thought a lot about how much time a person may want to spend reading or looking at something, so I didn't want these huge long sections of information that a viewer would get bored with. This is also why the UI and UX research was really helpful. In my website's "design metaphor", I'm using association: a blue, black and white colour scheme, coupled with imagery of VR and motherboards, that is often associated with gaming and computers and the life of a 26 year old Sims addict. And thank goddess for the Semantic Markup research because I have a long way to go with that. But the introduction put me on the right track. Not only is important for my website to be organised and easily consumable, but the same goes for the code behind it. The website still has a long way to go, there's a lot of refining that it needs and there are areas that I can see I can do better in (like how many rectangular grids can she actually have??).

Reflection: Website Iteration Two

This iteration focused on the JavaScript we've learned so far. There is still more HTML and CSS refining that I would like to get to, but this got overtaken by the new content I needed to add to the site being the JavaScript implementation and critical writings. Watching Andre's tutorial on generating random quotes is what inspired the 'random' video game idea (also inspired by Video Game Name Generator). It felt fitting to have something like this in my site given the field I'm in. Like with most coding languages however, I feel like my lack of knowledge really limits me. And watching tutorials are helpful but sometimes give me slight imposter syndrome. What was unfortunate about a section of my JavaScript was the contact form I created. It works if the email address is my own, so it won't accept anyone elses. But even if I put my own in, I don't get the message. I think it's got to do with a security restriction between Google and the SMTP I'm using. So work will need to be done in order to get this properly working.

My best and most meaningful part of this iteration though was the UI and UX reflection. I felt I learned a lot from it, especially deepening my understanding of both concepts and their important roles and uses. I had to really consider where my differences were, and to dig as deep as realising the effects the scan's dpi had on the image of the wireframe's quality made me feel like I was thinking proactively about nuanced aspects of the site.
