Information Geographies

Critical Writing

This critical reflection will dissect and discuss Towards a study of information geographies: (im)mutable augmentations and a mapping of the geographies of information by Mark Graham, Stefano De Sabbata, and Matthew A. Zook.

Aims:

Their paper investigated how information geographies have and are influenced by their own geographies of access, participation, and representation. They called these components geographic distributors.

Methodology:

Graham et al used multiple bespoke data and statistics, mostly quantitative and empirical data, to construe a broad survey that showcases a range of uneven geographies in access, participation, and representation. They used this method to analyse and report on three sections of their paper: geographies of access and enablement, geographies of participation and geographies of representation.

Observations and Results:

Geographies of access and enablement

This section investigated who and from where has access to the “technologies and services essential for digital communication, participation, and representation” (Graham et al 2015). They compiled data on internet users by country and compared that to the cost of broadband versus the average income. The data collected for internet users by country shows the global north has the most “internet penetration” than the global south, yet the cost of broadband data is most expensive in mainly Africa. They conclude this section with noting that the inability to connect to internet likely means participation and contribution becomes impossible. Access and enablement are heavily determined by affordability. But with affordability comes the not so hidden costs that Graham et. al could have included in their observation; internet access requires electricity which also must be paid for. It requires a device to be accessed on (which will also affect the effectiveness of participation) which has either been bought or is on some sort of contract. These are possibly some of the “contextual causes of these uneven geographies” (102) that need to be consider when furthering this investigation.

Geographies of participation

This section explores where digital information is created and produced. They sourced data from domain names, GitHub users and repositories, and Wikipedia to access “location[s] of digital content” (Graham et al 93 2015). Most domains are registered in Europe and North America which contrasts against most internet users in the global north. Thus, “large internet populations do not automatically translate into large numbers of domain registrations” (Graham et al 2015:95). GitHub statistics were able to show areas that are collaboratively developing code. Wikipedia contributions showed where content is being created from and furthermore how much is localised. The results of their data show that “geographies of participation matter are tied to how content and locales or places is created in different parts of the world” (97).

Geographies of representation

Graham et al use Google Search, OpenStreetMap and the Semantic Web to show the digital geographies of representation. There are regions, like the US, that have the most mentions in Google's index. They felt that a lot of representation happens when organisations invest or show interest in another place - like the EU creating a geographical information structure in Nepal. Something like this also happens because of the media and social media's geocoding. The paper up until this point portrays that the (im)mutable augmentations (unchanging and changing expansions) that are within information geographies are heavily influenced by the chain of who has access and is enabled, therefore who is participating and thus who is represented. And according to the results, the global north-west and much of northern Europe lead in all three categories. Thus, the global mid-south experiences the least of any of these categories as well as partially the global north-east.

Critique, Reflection and Conclusions

The paper, as the authors have acknowledged, lacks in thorough global evidence. Not only is it written in English, but it was researched in English. One of the biggest discrepancies because of this is seen in the Wikipedia research - “we were only able to use data about the English version of Wikipedia because the method of geolocating user profiles involved parsing unstructured English-language text” (Graham et. al 2015:97). Had they included bi-lingual researchers (or even translators) who could investigate the non-English web territory, they may have found different results for the middle-east and north-east regions.

What is most striking about this paper is their note on the work that needs to be done to begin to understand “the contextual causes of these uneven geographies” (Graham et. al 2015:102). Geographies of representation is a great place to explore race, religion, gender and identity, communities like LGBTQIA+ and intersectionality. What platforms do access and enablement afford participation? What are the rules and regulations of these platforms that protect those participation as lack of would perhaps lead to even less participation? One example of this is a Twitch streamer who was banned for hate speech and moved over to another streaming platform called DLive to continue his agenda of spreading the word of God (where the hate speech was underlined by telling other players, mostly children and in one particular case a Muslim child, that they were going to hell). DLive came under critique in 2020 when they changed their Twitter bio to “All Lives Matter” and other agenda's lead them to be accused of supporting and encouraging white supremacist and nationalist ideals (Bergengruen 2020 and Kurtis Connor 2021). Thus, in this case, certain participation shows how it can undermine and aim to silence participation of underrepresented and often discriminated communities. It is a doubled edged sword.

There is also evidence of internet infrastructure that is deeply saturated with colonialism. The following figure is taken from James Bridle's interview (Verso Books 2018) about his book A New Dark Age.

This image depicts Mediterranean Undersea Cables that supply internet. Cables that flow from England end in parts of India, a former British colony in terms of “physical” colonisation. Spain to this day holds a “protectorate” over Morocco and equips them with internet cables. In this scenario, if Morocco had to revolt and fight for full independence, would their internet supply be severed? Is this a modern-day form of control?

These are two avenues that could be explored in the contextualising work Graham et al. speak of. Their research and observations are good throughways to interrogating the contextual settings of why these geographies of information are the way they are under a localised lens. These are important aspects of the world wide web to consider as they will influence the way in which we as digital designers use the internet to research and produce information.
