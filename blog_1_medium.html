McLuhan Reflection: Mediums as Messages

The McLuhan reading teaches us to think about how mediums become messages, how they influence those messages (in the methods a medium will use to communicate a message), and possibly how those messages can even then become their own mediums. Mediums have social, political, economic, personal consequences - affecting our ideas of humanity. This reading made me think of Stuart Hall's (1997) systems of representation - how signifier and signified make up a sign and how these signs sometimes create a universal language; a message. Within this, mediums are signs which contain messages exclusive to the content of the medium.

An advert that appears on television holds a message in a different meaning than if that same message was conveyed in the medium of print. Adverts that appear on TV or radio indicate a connotative message that the company can afford an actress, a production team and the studio to shoot in. This may appear as a hidden message to the viewer or listener that the company is more reputable because of the 'luxury' they can afford to advertise their business. But compared to the possible expense of advertising on a billboard on William Nicol, it's hard to say if that connotative or hidden meaning holds much weight. In the case of the billboard medium, messages have to be aptly and skillfully designed to convey a strong message to those driving by - as the message needs to be received as quickly as possible. Does the medium then inflict the message with urgency? In this scenario, we see that the medium of the billboard influences the advert's message greatly in its creation and deliverance, purely from the message the medium conveys itself.

There was a lot to unpack in the reading; these were two comments that I thought I'd add to the discussion: His point on the American Revolution, that it "had no medieval legal institutions to discard or to root out, apart from monarchy" (McLuhan, 1964, p.206), paired with his sentiment that England is yet to undergo their revolution, feels like it missed an opportunity to highlight the medieval legalities that the American Civil Rights movement aimed to root out. I know he's speaking on De Tocqueville's work and is focusing on technology but I feel this is important: The mediums of the movement, which I feel stretch all the way from posters to even schools (like in Little Rock), are such good examples of messages becoming mediums themselves. The protest at Little Rock Central Highschool can be encapsulated as a medium because it was a platform for white supremacists to spew their ideologies (their message), but the resulting message that came from their actions - black students being escorted by the military - became a medium for civil rights activists to further their message. I bring this up because McLuhan published this work in 1964, several years in to the CRM - a legal, political and social revolution in my opinion.

"The grammar of print cannot help to construe the message of oral and nonwritten culture and institutions" (McLuhan, 1964, p.206) - this made me think of a reading on orality and oral cultures and how we learned about it in relation to storytelling. A spoken story (at first a message) told by a storyteller (arguably the medium) becomes itself a medium within the listener's memory (Ong, 2002). The story is now a medium in memory as its message is the contents that the listener remembers. The only change here is that the memory is the medium instead of the storyteller. Recalling elements of the story would then translate into a message that is relevant for the listeners current activity (like if they were talking to someone about a story they once heard or were applying a principle like forgiveness that they learned through the narrative). I feel the same process would happen with a written and read story, just that the messages would have different results. In this case, the reader is isolated and reads the message to themselves. Messages can sometimes be stronger when they come from someone else, like an oral storyteller. When we internalise messages by ourselves, we can only use our perspectives to understand that message. And this changes the meaning of a message greatly. Whereas sometimes, it's helpful to understand a message through the perspective of someone else, someone external. This long train of thought is to dance on the roles of the medium which, in this case, are minds. Our minds are mediums because of the way in which they house a message and what that message will then mean to us when it is recalled in the future. I could go on and on, but hopefully you sort of get the picture.

Much like stories and storytellers, what this reading ultimately made me think of is games and game-makers. What messages will my games hold if they are influenced by the medium they are played through? The medium, as "any extension of ourselves" (McLuhan, 1964, p.203), shows the impact of scenarios like using mouse and keyboard as opposed to a controller. Technically and figuratively, the mouse, keyboard, and controller are extensions of ourselves and enable our bodies to create input. They are gateways to the medium (the game) but they also effect the reception of the message. Why does driving vehicles feel better on controller for some people? It is because the joysticks mimic a similar notion to steering? GTA and Rocket League, to me, are 100% better experienced through using a controller because steering the vehicles feels more natural than on mouse and keyboard. Thus, in a more personal opinion, Rocket League as a medium better expresses its message - soccer but with cars - through the help and enhancement of a controller. This avenue took a turn towards how we access a message from a medium, but hopefully also shed more light on the powerful influence mediums have on our choices - how our messages are influenced by the medium we choose because of the affordances given by that medium.

Trying to conclude all of these different avenues may hopefully be done if I've made at least one point clear: mediums contain messages, but mediums themselves are also messages. And there is much that affects how these messages that mediums convey are created and understood. With that in mind, it will always be important to be critical of the medium you chose to convey a message, as some mediums will do better than others.
