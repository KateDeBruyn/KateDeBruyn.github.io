Design Metaphors and Digital Information

Design Metaphors in and for digital information

In any instance of processing digital information, User Interface and User Experience form an integral part. Within their design lie metaphors - key elements, like concepts, terms and images, that represent data in order for a user to comprehend, consume, recall and command (Marcus 1998, pp 129). These elements can be portrayed through actions, procedures and/or images and also exist to utilise prior knowledge so that a user may instantaneously understand the information presented by the interface (The Audiopedia 2017).

Using metaphors to understand digital information

Humans are always processing information. As I type this, I am drawing my ideas and thoughts from the literature I have just engaged with in order to write a reflection on it; I am processing information I've just come across in order to execute another process where I can reflect on the information. On top of that, there is the process of thought to type; I am processing my thoughts and opinions through the act of typing. On top of that, the machine I am typing on is also undergoing its own process in order to show me the letters that I'm typing on a screen, so I am processing how the words are coming across the screen too. This is just one out of many avenues of processing information. Humans also process information through organisation. Association is one way in which information can be organised in order to understand, and design metaphors enable us to do this. We can draw on a key element such as 'attribute' show this process. Humans give people, objects, places, events, and so on, attributes in order to understand them. WIRED (2014) touches on this idea by illustrating studies that have shown how physical warmth can link to emotional warmth. Warmth is an attribute humans may give to a fire or a jersey. But warmth is also an attribute they may give to someone's voice or a feeling. A studied showed that romantic movies were more sought after during a cold front - these people were finding the emotional warmth in romance in order to combat the physical lack of warmth from the weather. And as a result, an association about romantic movies and cold weather can be made, the association being that of warmth. How does this metaphor help process information? It is possible that viewers may pay more attention to the movie because the setting and context it is being watched in is 'right'. Thus, the information from the movie is being better comprehended and consumed. Perhaps even the controls, the User Interface, of the streaming platform, or the DVD's settings were even easier to grasp because of the favorable condition they were being engaged in. As Marcus (1998:128) states, "metaphors may achieve their effectiveness through associations of organization" but it is important to know how these associations are formed and what kinds of information they organise. Using design metaphors in something like User Interface, helps a user to understand digital information. It is also a good place to highlight the importance and use of pre-existing knowledge. How did humans come to understand digital technology? This may be a far-fetched opinion, but perhaps it started with the invention of electricity as many of our digital technologies depend on it. Without taking too deep of a dive into the historical nuances of "digital", "technology", and "digital technology", electricity and electrical inventions date as far back as 600BC. However, it is only around 1925 that electrical inventions made their way into user's homes - this being the type of lightbulb that Thomas Eddison invented nearly 40 years prior (National Park Service 2015). I would like to imagine that this is possibly where most humans (users) began to learn that an electrical switch turns something electric on and off. This action can situate itself in the sets of functions that are verbs in visual-verbal communication (Marcus), most likely within select or evaluate. Learning how a light switch functions means that humans can use that knowledge when they come across other perceived switches. We learn that switch indicates a state change. Even though it is a button click different to a traditional light switch, we learn how to switch a device on or off. An on/off switch or button on a device is part of its User Interface design and communicates digital information (turning on or shutting down a system). This is a fundamental concept (on or off), sometimes even with the use of imagery, that makes the system's information (can use or cannot use) comprehensive to the user, in other words, a design metaphor.
