Reflection: UI and UX Design

When starting this UI and UX analysis and reflection, I realised that I have always understood these terms with a Graphic Design lens. Probably from having studied it for a year and encountering those terms for really the first time there. Back then though, I wasn't developing websites. The readings, lectures, and other course content we've been engaging with this year have directed my understanding of UI and UX towards the study of digital information. Thus, this analysis reflects on previous critical writings I've submitted as well as comparisons of my work against the course content we've engaged with.

In my Design Metaphors critical writing, I reflected on how these metaphors allow users to consume the information in front of them through key elements that Aaron Marcus (1998) defines as the concepts, terms and images that represent data, functions, tasks, organizations, and people. An example of this is the 'continue reading…' element I included in the main blog page, assigned to each available blog. It could be argued that, at least in English web development, that 'continue…' or 'continue reading…' is a universal concept used to allow users to, quite literally, continue reading an article or blog that sparks their interest. It is a universal concept that represents a function that enabled users to choose how and what they want to interact with on my website.

This also helpful in providing information on what the user can expect from a blogs page. I did feel that using some imagery would also help the user better identify what each blog entails. I found using images also helps the user understand the digital information in front of them by visualising a topic in this scenario. I had to edit the UX of the page in order to map the changes I wanted to make to the UI - adding images to each blog.

The main blog page lacked imagery in my first submission, something that I've now changed because it is another key element that also represents the data of the listed blogs. And while some people respond better to the written word, others respond better to imagery and visuals. So going back to this critical writing was important for me to realise those nuances and change my design choices - my UI and UX design choices.

When comparing my website to the keys points that Peta Goldberg (2022) presented in her lecture, I noticed that I had but also lacked depth in my initial deployment. While UX makes interfaces useful, my navigation bar's shortfall was in that it was not anchored during a scroll down the page; meaning the user had to scroll all the way back to the top to use the nav bar. That doesn't make it seem very useful or user friendly.

Anchoring the nav bar was also important in making the user feel grounded as opposed to lost. Navigation is an important aspect of UI and UX design, and having anchoring elements supports that notion as the user is given the ability to always 'find' themselves on the page. I needed to introduce this to my blog and projects pages too. I have reflected on those changes here. From tweaking and refining the anchoring and spatial awareness notions of my website, I thought about what connection the user may be making with my website. Furthermore, what connection they may be making with me. I thought of this quite literally at first, so I began with finding a way the user could get in touch with me.

I included a 'get-in-contact'wireframe to rework my UX to accomplish these 'connectivity' goals I had and to also introduce a call to action functionality. Before that, it felt like the UX of my website, like in my first wireframe, was accomplishing the goal of showing my UI design choices. I felt like my wireframe represented more of my UI thoughts than it did my UX, and when I came across a reading by Mariia Orlova (2016), my feelings were confirmed. Wireframes should be “stripped of any visual design or branding elements” and should “define the hierarchy [and communication] of items” based on what we deem the user's needs are (Orlova 2016: 24). I spoke about graphic design, and I've completely forgotten about basic design principles as well as completely missing those points in Peta's lecture notes. These just have to be applied to the digital information and content that I'm trying to portray in my website. So, I need to work on and better my wireframes with all of this in mind. A rework is needed, and I can see it in my lack of simply not knowing what is important to add or what do to next.

I was putting a lot of focus on my UI which included hyperfocusing on grids. My website's UI is largely and almost solely influenced by grids, and thus, its UX is built and formed around grid structures.

When thinking about how Peta's lecture and Marcus' reading connect, my use of the CSS grids came up. I used grids on. every. page. On Peta's side, it speaks to how UI deals with beauty and aesthetics and how using grids introduces a symmetrical balance to the page. These aspects tie in with Marcus' (1998) point on how metaphors achieve effectiveness through association of organisation or operation. A grid is organisational in terms of its structure - it can be perceived as a container and thus is appropriate for a user to find or assume to find information within it. Organisation and symmetry work together to make the information more easily consumable. Or at least that's just what I think by looking at my website at this point. However, what I make up for in organisation and readability, I feel I lack in creativity.

I also may want to change my UI. I thought about this idea of having a theme change every two months or so that is based off my current interest - right now it's Komi Can't Communicate (anime). I feel like this will personalise the website even more and will be a bit of an easter egg moment to those who engage with the site. It will also keep me working on the site. I am happy with what I started with and submitted in the first assignment, but I feel like I can refine it in a new direction. Something that I am definitely looking forward to.

In an article I came across by Matt Ellis (2018), I found it interesting how he illustrated that the fields of web design, web development, UI, and UX are all interdependent. I felt I knew this, but he states it quite well in that, “it's less about which fields handle which tasks, and more about how each field considers the same task from a different point-of-view.” He relates this point to loading times; web design may show that the content is too much or too complicated, web development encourages one to see what files can be compressed, UI encourages the controls to be simplified so that the user's learnability and subsequent input is instantaneous, and UX enables the developer to prioritise loading times for the index page. This made me think of my style guide section and a blog post where I included a video. I feel the blog post was fine, but the quality of my style guide section felt off. I realised I scanned the wireframe draft at 200dpi when screens run at a traditional 72dpi. Instead of scanning it with a lower dpi, I went into illustrator and tried to compress it by changing the dimensions and compressing it to 72dpi. While it did lower the file size, I sacrificed quality and therefore the UI to some regard. And I did this all so that it could fit into the grid - content in my web design choices that's possibly too complicated.
