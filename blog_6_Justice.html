Internet, Society and Design Justice

When first thinking about the internet, society, and design justice topic, I immediately thought about the critical writing I did on information geographies. Their research clearly showed areas where design and digital injustice is prevalent - entire regions where, though they may have access to the internet, the data itself is almost unaffordable. Regions, like India, where there are many internet users, but few registered domains (Graham et al 2015: 94). The empirical and quantitative data collected for this research showed uneven geographies in internet access and enablement, subsequent digital participation, and digital representation. There was a throughline that intrinsically linked the three sections in that one almost always lead to the other. A person who has a disadvantageous internet access (either it is too expensive or perhaps not readily accessible in their home) is less likely to participate online and thus, sees the space for their digital representation shrink. Their article offered research that could be looked at through a lens of digital equality. In the practice of digital equality, we are engaging with “the analog and digital aspects of political, social, cultural and economic dimensions in society” (Sambuli in Magnoni 2019). The aim of this practice is to close the gap between the unequal and the privileged through technology - and this could be done so through understanding and investigating design justice.

In her talk Justice by Design, Antionette Carroll explains areas of design injustice. Injustice can be found in design that is systemic, such as the design choices of media coverage. It is seen in what headliner they may choose, or font they may use, the image they decide on, the edit of the video; like in some of the media coverage we saw around the Black Lives Matter protests. Digital articles and videos circulated different arguments and skewed perspectives, but in each of them were systemic design choices that show that “systems of oppression, injustices, and inequities are designed” (Carroll 2017). Costanza-Chock (in Geyser 2021) leans into the “design of sociotechnical systems”, systems whose design technologically and socially intersect and thus brings together the injustices of each. And design justice is intrinsically intersectional - from who or what the designed information is about to who or what has access to that information. In order to flesh out the point I'm trying to make, I will use a current project I am working on as an example.

Flanagan and Nissenbaum (2007) offer a methodology game designers may use when designing games with strong social themes and implications. Their article situates design justice (and injustice) from the position of the designer. They call upon the designer to hold themselves and their projects accountable, against a methodology that helps root out design injustice. They call it the Values at Play (VAP) approach. It encourages designers and design teams to discover and define the values their game will have, translate these values into the structure, the architecture, of the game and verify that the values were received in the way they should have been. The Sims 4 Activism Mod that I am working on will deeply benefit from this methodology. In short, I am creating a mod that allows players to make their Sims activists so that they can fight for social and socio-political causes. As Flanagan and Nissenbaum (2007: 183) state, “sometimes, values are expressed explicitly in the functional definition of a system”. The values of the activism mod may be clear to some: values of equality, justice, inclusion. The issue that lies here though is that these are my values. I haven't compared these to or against other people's values and views on activism. Some may feel including cooperation and security is a must. This could be found out through playtesting (verification). Another point made by Flanagan and Nissenbaum (2007: 184), is that a “game's reward system is a crucial mechanism for expressing the game's goals and values”. My mod has an aspect where Sims who complete the activism aspiration track receive what is know as a reward trait - a personality trait that can only be gained through completing aspirations. So, the question for me is, what would be a meaningful and rewarding trait to obtain? How will this trait embody the mod's goals and values?

Carroll (2017) challenges that the definitions for design and designer should be reworked. They should be based on the question: does the solution I am designing create a better world or at least a better outcome? And from here we can also draw from the principles of design justice (Geyser 2021): the designer must be a facilitator and offer solutions that sustain, heal, and empower rather than exploit. In terms of me being the designer for the mod, I must ask myself the questions (and answer them): does this mod facilitate activist values? Or does indulge me of a white saviour complex? Will players find the mod healing and empowering or will their real-life experiences feel exploited by me? And to bring it back to the initial connections made in the writing, who will have access to this mod? It is already exclusive to The Sims players. Who has access to the game? Since it isn't a free game, it's essentially a luxury. And furthermore, who will be able to participate and thus be represented? My mod may not bridge the gaps found in digital inequality, but at least it does offer me a place to investigate my place and position as a designer, something I feel we should all be doing.
