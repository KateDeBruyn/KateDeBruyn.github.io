Capitalist Creativity

Gill and Pratt (2008) have highlighted that a class within the global sphere of entrepreneurs has emerged, and it is one assembled by creatives, mostly digital, such as artists, content creators, designers and the like. The creative class has risen with the exponential nature of technology and the digitilisation of the workplace. This essay will aim to define and situate the creative class as they have been deemed “model entrepreneurs” (Gill & Pratt 2008: 2) and then highlight the effects that the labour of the creative class undergoes within capitalism, digitilisation and the advancement of technology. It will then conclude with possible ways in which the creative class may free themselves from the effects and inflictions of neoliberal capitalism.

Changes in modern societys workplaces are eminent and ever growing. They are accompanied, or perhaps propelled, by an incessant need for labourers to rapidly and adaptively learn new technologies to keep up with industry standards. These labourers form a mostly digital or online workforce whose prerequisite into the online industry is creativity; the ability to be creative and think and work creatively. Gill and Pratt (2008: 3) define these labourers as “cultural and creative workers” who form a creative entrepreneurial class; model entrepreneurs who have been identified by governments and industries as an easily exploitable workforce because of their subjectivity to unstable and lawfully ill-supported career paths. It is in the governments and the industries favour to manifest such a workforce as accountability, risk and responsibility fall entirely on the single creative labourer (Gill & Pratt 2008: 4). This individuality also promises a disunity within the class, however that will be addressed later. Gill and Pratt (2008: 2) situate the creative class as “centre stage” perhaps because of their most telling attributes of modern-day capitalism. They identify them as the latest precariat that expose the subtleties of capitalisms and neoliberalisms inflictions of instability and disunion. Creative labourers, the precariat, under a critical lens find themselves at the epicentre of labour exploitation and as Gill and Pratt (2008: 4) posit, possible “new political subjectivity.”

This essay will reflect on a portion of the creative class that is made up of digital and online workers; anyone whose career or form of income comes from their online persona. For example, a musician whose music is on Soundcloud but has minimal or no compensation due to not being signed with a company. A graphic designer who receives employment based off clients viewing their online portfolio through Instagram. The underpinning attribute of these scenarios is the precariousness of employment. However, the creative class is misled by being sold the ideology of freedom through creative work; the creative is free to work as and when they please as opposed to a rigid status quo job (Leary 2018: 30). The creative is told they are following passion which means they should overlook the free labour that employers, industries, and even government exploit. The graphic designer who uses Instagram as a professional platform enables Instagrams profits through the interactions a post will gain. The time, effort and energy, the free labour Instagram exploits, that goes into producing content the designer can post goes unpaid. Nor can it be billed once or if they receive a commission. Time, effort and energy can be understood as “immaterial labour” (Gill & Pratt 2008). They are attributes that are not regarded as work yet still produce something consumable.

A capitalist notion found within the creative class online workplace is what Casilli (2017: 3943) explains as “platform capitalism”. It has an intrinsic link to the class subjectivity to government rule and politics. The precariat is subjected to the “social dynamics of marginalisation and disempowerment” (Casilli 2017: 3943) as the pattern of their cheap or unpaid labour aligns with the paradigm of low-cost labour found through gendered or racialised minority groups. Platform capitalism sees the creative labourer as similar to the minority labourer for various reasons; the lack of lawful and monetary support or accepting underpaid or even unpaid (such as an internship) employment and the shared “insecure forms of living” (Gill & Pratt 2008: 5). Platform capitalism is a liked political agenda as it inflicts ways to subdue and divide the creative class. The division comes partly in the form of competition. A content creators social media must be more active, have more views and likes than their competitors for them to receive the job instead. Organising and uniting may be unfavourable. A small example of this effect was seen in the making of Click; a group of six Australian YouTubers who banded together to create content. They went as far as to move in with each other, but over time separated due to it being difficult to make new and authentic content while they were constantly around each other. Though this is not an example of an organised union, it does portray why content creators are encouraged to stay individual; their content is personal and unique and their success can thus be owed to themselves. Finally, the creative class find themselves at the mercy of the unpredictable essence of their online workplaces. Not only is employment inauspicious, but the workspaces are precarious too. The upgrades platforms make to their software inflict “labor requirements of online persona management” (Scolere et al. 2018: 7) that constantly call for the creative class to readjust. The creative class must put in more time and effort into learning or rethinking strategies to stay accessible and relevant within the platform. There are little to no laws regulating platform updates. An illustrator who once got majority of their interactivity through feed posts may see a dip when Instagram implemented stories and reels in an update. The illustrator must now learn new ways to produce and publish content to gain the same and more interactivity they had before. There are no regulatory laws that protect the illustrators labour, or more so, their immaterial labour.

The anti-capitalist theories of Michael Hardt and Antonio Negri are pulled into the argument as their belief is that creative “workers use of technology exceeds the capacity of capital to control” (Gill & Pratt 2008: 12) them offer ways in which Autonomous Marxism potentially overthrows capitalism. As this essay focused on the inflictions of neoliberalist capitalism on the creative class, the model entrepreneurial pedestal they have been put on, perhaps a jumpstart to change the discourse is for the class to become aware and well versed in their role within society; what they could do to better support themselves and the class as a whole.
